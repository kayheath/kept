# Product Brief: Kept

> A calming, offline desktop todo app — built as a gift for one person. Drawn from the 2026-06-15 brainstorming session via the bmad-product-brief coaching path.

## Executive Summary

**Kept** is a calming, offline todo app built for one person: a bright, capable man who gets overwhelmed by his own to-do list. He sets a high bar for himself, so every task silently becomes a non-negotiable *must* — and seeing them all at once, laid bare on a whiteboard, doesn't organize his day so much as measure the gap between who he is and who he thinks he should be by nightfall. The result is shutdown, not progress.

This app refuses the usual todo-app contract. Instead of confronting him with everything that's left, it celebrates everything he's *done*, holds the rest of the list quietly in trust, and threads his partner's voice — pre-written notes of encouragement and love — through his day. It inverts the metaphor from *burndown* (debt you chip away at) to *build-up* (proof you accumulate). It is, underneath the task-tracking, an emotional-regulation tool: a way for one person's steadiness and care to be present even when she can't be in the room.

It is built offline-first and personal by design — not a product for a market, but a gift for a particular person, shaped entirely around how *he* works and what calms *him*. Its single law: the love in it is never conditional, never withheld, never earned.

## The Problem

He doesn't have a productivity problem. He has an *exposure* problem. The tasks themselves are manageable; it's **seeing them all at once** that triggers the spiral.

Today his system is a whiteboard — the worst possible medium for him. It does only one thing: stand there as a list of what he needs to do. It can't hold anything in trust, and it offers nothing back when he finishes — erasing a task gives him no real payoff, just a slightly shorter list of obligations still staring at him. So standing in front of it on a hard morning, three things happen, every time:

- He tells his partner how overwhelmed and stressed he is.
- He asks *her* for the motivation to keep going.
- He finishes things — and gives himself no credit for any of it.

That last one is the quiet tragedy. The whiteboard is built to display obligation, not accomplishment — so his wins never land anywhere. His emotional center of gravity stays fixed on what's *left*, never on what he's *done*. He clears tasks into a void and feels no lighter for it.

The cost isn't just unfinished tasks. It's a capable person who feels perpetually behind, who can't bank his own accomplishments — and a partner who has become his sole, on-call source of motivation, love that can't scale past the moments she's physically present.

## The Solution

The app does for him what the whiteboard never could: it carries the weight, so he doesn't have to hold the whole day in his head at once.

**A morning with it looks like this.** He opens the app and sees *one* thing to focus on — not twelve. Below it, growing, is the pile of what he's already done today, with his partner's notes tucked among the wins. The rest of the day isn't gone — it's held safely, one calm click away behind a *"show me my day"* gesture that opens gently: no red, no counts shouting at him, just a few small, finishable groups chunked by time of day instead of one intimidating ranked list. He's always in control of how much he sees; the app *suggests* calm — it never locks him out of what he wants.

**Adding a task costs nothing.** He types a few words and he's done — no required fields, no due-date interrogation, no planning ritual. The app quietly sorts it where it belongs. For an over-planner, planning is itself a stressor, so the app removes that tax: he brain-dumps, it organizes.

**Finishing a task is a moment, not a checkbox.** Completion is a slow, deliberate gesture — the task drifts softly into the done-pile, and one of his partner's notes blooms in. Sometimes it's everyday warmth; once in a while, a rare surprise; and now and then, a note tied to something real (the gym, a hard work day). The notes are pre-written and saved on the device, so her encouragement is always there — never dependent on her being reachable, never dependent on him having *earned* it. Especially on the bad days, the love shows up anyway.

These pieces don't arrive in stages — he receives one complete, finished app. Woven into that whole: a gentle two-date model that separates a real external *deadline* from the *do-date* he intends to work on something (grad-school structure without the deadline glaring daily); a tone that shifts with the clock — encouraging by day, all pressure dissolved into acceptance by night; and a journal where his wins and her words stack into a keepsake he can revisit when he needs it.

## What Makes This Different

The advantage here is **intimacy, not technology.** Nothing in this app is technically hard to build — any competent team could ship the features. What no team could do is build it for *him*. A todo app sold to a market has to serve millions, so it averages everyone into blandness and defaults to the loudest possible design: red badges, counts, streaks, urgency. This app is calibrated to exactly one nervous system. Every default is the *opposite* of the industry's, on purpose:

- **It celebrates done, not pending.** The entire genre is built on the burndown — the anxious shrinking list. This one builds *up*, and keeps his accomplishments in the foreground where his own mind never does.
- **The love is unconditional — and that's a design law, not a feature.** His partner's voice is never disappointed, never withheld, never something he has to earn by completing tasks. It shows up *especially* on the days he gets nothing done. No commercial app would dare make affection non-contingent on engagement; that's the whole point of this one.
- **It separates the deadline from the do-date.** Real external pressure (a hard due date) is held quietly in the background as a safety net, while the day organizes around when he *intends* to act — structure without the deadline glaring at him daily.

The honest version: this could never be a business, and it doesn't want to be. Its defensibility is that it would make no sense to anyone but him.

## Who This Serves

**Him — the only user who matters.** A bright, capable, high-standards man (currently in grad school) who turns his to-do list into a list of *musts* and shuts down under the weight of seeing it all. He needs to act on one thing while still trusting the rest is handled, to be reminded of what he's accomplished, and to feel encouraged rather than judged. Success for him: he feels motivated instead of buried, and he starts giving himself the credit the whiteboard never gave him.

**Her — the partner, the second user nobody designs for.** She is, today, his on-call source of motivation. This app lets her care scale: her encouragement, written once, is there on his desktop every hard morning — including the ones she can't be there for. Success for her: she watches the overwhelm quiet down, and her love does some of its work even when she's not in the room.

## Success Criteria

This is a gift, not a business, so the only real measure is whether *he* changes. The signals, drawn directly from the three things he does at the whiteboard today:

- **He reaches for the app for motivation** — at least sometimes — instead of only reaching for her. The app starts shouldering some of the load.
- **He gives himself credit, unprompted.** A "look what I got done today" where before there was only "look what's left." The done-pile is doing its job.
- **The overwhelm quiets.** He vents stress about his list less often, or less intensely. The bad morning gets gentler.
- **He keeps choosing it.** Past the novelty of a gift, he still opens it on his own — and eventually adds his own tasks, the sign he trusts it to hold the weight.

None of these require interrogating him. They're things she'll simply notice. Honest caveat: this is a sample size of one, measured by feel — and that's the right instrument for this product.

## Scope

**In — the complete, finished app he receives:**

- A calm home screen: one task in focus, a growing done-pile, his partner's notes woven through, and the full day always one gentle click away — scoped to today, chunked by time of day, never a wall of tasks.
- Frictionless capture with automatic sorting — no required fields, no planning tax.
- The note vault: pre-written notes (everyday warmth, rare surprises, a few tied to real anchors), surfaced by a timing engine that reads the shape of his day. Always unconditional.
- The slow completion ritual and the day journal (wins + her words, kept).
- The two-date model (deadline vs. do-date), day/night tone shift, and the empty-day invitation.
- **A stocked vault of real notes, written by her.** This is content, not code — and it is the soul of the app. The app is not "done" until the vault is full of her actual words, sorted into everyday warmth / rare surprises / anchor-specific. Treated as a first-class deliverable, not an afterthought.
- Fully offline / local-first, built as a **desktop application**. Everything lives on his computer; nothing depends on a connection or on her being reachable.

**Explicitly out — and out on purpose:**

- No accounts, no login, no cloud, no sync. It's his, on his machine.
- No social features, no sharing, no leaderboards.
- No streaks, badges, counts, or any gamified urgency — these are the exact mechanics the app exists to reject.
- No live messaging. Her presence is *pre-written and saved*, by design — so it never depends on her being online.
- No analytics or tracking. Nobody is measuring him.
